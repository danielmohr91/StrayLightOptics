Research

What Is Stray Light?

Stray light is unwanted light – unwanted because it degrades system performance.
It might be thought of as optical noise. Usually, we think about impact on the detector of the optical system (loss of contrast, false signals, etc), but it also includes light that lands on sensitive components and results in thermal damage (both optics in high power lasers and DUV systems and mech structure). Stray light is any light that adversely impacts system performance, either through direct degradation of image quality or damage to part of the system.

Where does it come from?

  • Ghost reflections (Fresnel reflections from optical surfaces)
  • Scatter from mechanical components and roughness on optical components
  • Thermal emission from the instrument itself
  • Unwanted diffraction orders
  • Contamination (particulates, etc)

Why is it important?

Stray light can degrade system performance. It can prevent optical systems from detecting or measuring their intended targets. Left unmanaged/untreated, the optical instrument will perform at a less than it’s full potential.

How is it evaluated?

Stray light signal is usually estimated using optical analysis software, usually a non-sequential raytrace program (examples). Given the opto-mechanical geometry, optical properties, source properties, the user computes how much / where the stray light affects the system. For simple situations, it is sometimes possible to estimate stray light using basic radiometry without the use of raytrace software.

How is it managed / fixed?

Specifics depend on details of the optical system, but general mitigation techniques include blocking the stray light by mechanical means, preventing the source of stray light from being illuminated, moving components, improving coatings, surface treatments, reducing contamination, improving processes (polishing), active cooling, and calibration (signal processing).
